Lecture 5 The Steady-State Approximation

In the previous chapter, we saw that for the mechanism A + B \(\rightleftharpoons\) C \(\rightarrow\) D, we can write down the differential rate equations for every species, but we cannot derive an exact analytical expression for the overall rate of reaction in terms of the reactant concentrations alone. To make progress, we applied the pre-equilibrium approximation: we assumed \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\), so that the first step has time to equilibrate before significant product forms. This gave us a tractable rate law — but one that is valid only in that specific limit.

This is a common situation in chemical kinetics. For many multi-step mechanisms, the differential rate equations are coupled — the rate of change of one species depends on the concentrations of others — and there is no general analytical solution. To extract useful rate laws from such mechanisms, we need to make approximations. The pre-equilibrium approximation is one such approach. In this chapter, we introduce another: the steady-state approximation (SSA), which applies when the reactive intermediate is short-lived and highly reactive.

5.1 The Idea Behind the Steady-State Approximation

A reactive intermediate is a species that is formed during a reaction and then consumed before the reaction is complete. It does not appear in the overall balanced equation. In many mechanisms, reactive intermediates are highly unstable: they are consumed almost as fast as they are produced, so their concentration remains small throughout the reaction.

If a reactive intermediate is consumed much faster than it is formed, two things follow. First, its concentration must remain small compared to those of the reactants and products — if it were to accumulate, the fast consumption step would rapidly remove the excess. Second, after a brief initial period during which the intermediate builds up from zero, its concentration reaches a nearly constant value — a steady state — because any small increase in concentration is immediately counteracted by the fast consumption step.

The steady-state approximation exploits this. For a reactive intermediate I whose concentration is approximately constant, \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t \approx 0\). Under the SSA, we therefore set \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\):

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = 0\]

This does not mean that \([\mathrm{I}] = 0\). The intermediate is present — it must be, because the reaction proceeds through it — but its concentration changes so slowly compared to the other species that we treat \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t\) as zero. This replaces a differential equation (which, together with the equations for the other species, forms a coupled system with no analytical solution) with an algebraic equation that we can solve by hand.

Figure 5.1 illustrates this for the consecutive reaction A \(\rightarrow\) B \(\rightarrow\) C when \(k_1 \ll k_2\). Because B is consumed much faster than it is formed, it never accumulates to a significant concentration. After a brief initial rise, \([\mathrm{B}]\) remains low and nearly constant — precisely the conditions under which the SSA is valid.

Figure 5.1: Concentration profiles for the consecutive reaction A → B → C when \(k_1 \ll k_2\). The intermediate B rises briefly to a low concentration and then remains nearly constant as A is consumed — this is the steady state. Because B is consumed (by the fast second step) much faster than it is formed (by the slow first step), it never accumulates to a significant concentration. The SSA approximates this behaviour by setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\).

5.2 Applying the SSA: A + B ⇌ C → D

Let us return to the mechanism we analysed using the pre-equilibrium approximation in the previous chapter:

\[\mathrm{A} + \mathrm{B} \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{D}\]

We want the overall rate of product formation, \(\nu = \mathrm{d}[\mathrm{D}]/\mathrm{d}t = k_2[\mathrm{C}]\). But C is a reactive intermediate that we cannot measure directly — we need to express \(\nu\) in terms of the stable species A and B.

The rate equations for each species are:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{A}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= -k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] + k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] \\ \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] - k_2[\mathrm{C}] \\ \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{D}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= k_2[\mathrm{C}] \end{align*}\]

These are coupled differential equations: the equation for \([\mathrm{C}]\) depends on \([\mathrm{A}]\) and \([\mathrm{B}]\), and the equations for \([\mathrm{A}]\) and \([\mathrm{B}]\) depend on \([\mathrm{C}]\). In general, coupled systems like this have no simple analytical solution — we would need to solve them numerically. This is the same problem we encountered in the previous chapter: we can write down the rate equations for any mechanism, but we cannot always solve them.

If C is a short-lived, reactive intermediate, however, we can apply the SSA. Setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\) gives:

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] - k_2[\mathrm{C}] = 0\]

This is now an algebraic equation — the coupled differential equation for \([\mathrm{C}]\) has been replaced by an expression we can rearrange directly. Solving for the steady-state concentration of C:

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] = (k_{-1} + k_2)[\mathrm{C}]\]

\[[\mathrm{C}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]}{k_{-1} + k_2}\]

The overall rate of product formation is then:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{D}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_2[\mathrm{C}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]}{k_{-1} + k_2} \tag{5.1} \end{equation}\]

This is the SSA rate law for this mechanism. It is second order overall (first order in A, first order in B), with an effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/(k_{-1} + k_2)\). This is an approximate result — it relies on the assumption that C reaches a steady state — but it gives us a closed-form rate law expressed entirely in terms of measurable species.

5.2.1 Limiting Cases

Equation (5.1) is a general approximate rate law. As we did for consecutive reactions in the previous chapter, we can examine what happens in particular limiting cases. The behaviour is controlled by the denominator \((k_{-1} + k_2)\) — specifically, by which of the two terms dominates.

Pre-equilibrium limit (\(k_{-1} \gg k_2\)):

When the reverse of the first step is much faster than the second step, \(k_{-1}\) is much larger than \(k_2\), so the denominator is dominated by \(k_{-1}\):

\[(k_{-1} + k_2) \approx k_{-1}\]

Substituting into Eqn. (5.1):

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]\]

This is exactly the pre-equilibrium rate law we derived in the previous chapter (Eqn. (4.4)). Physically, C reverts to A + B many times before occasionally proceeding to D. The first step has time to reach equilibrium, and the slow second step controls the overall rate. The effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\) depends on all three elementary rate constants.

Irreversible limit (\(k_2 \gg k_{-1}\)):

When the second step is much faster than the reverse of the first step, \(k_2\) is much larger than \(k_{-1}\), and the denominator is dominated by \(k_2\):

\[(k_{-1} + k_2) \approx k_2\]

Substituting into Eqn. (5.1):

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_2}[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] = k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]\]

Now the overall rate depends only on \(k_1\). Once C is formed, it proceeds to D so rapidly that reversion to A + B is negligible. The first step is made irreversible by the fast second step, even though it is intrinsically reversible. The first step is therefore rate-determining, and the rate law is simply the rate of the first elementary step.

Notice that the two limits give the same functional form (both are second order in A and B) but different effective rate constants: \(k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\) in the pre-equilibrium limit and \(k_1\) in the irreversible limit.

Figure 5.2 compares the SSA prediction with the exact numerical solution for this mechanism.

Figure 5.2: Exact concentration profiles for the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D (solid lines), together with the SSA prediction for [D] (dashed line). The intermediate C remains at low concentration throughout, consistent with the steady-state assumption. The SSA prediction closely matches the exact solution except at short times, where the intermediate has not yet reached its steady-state concentration and the approximation is least valid.

You should be able to: Apply the steady-state approximation to the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D by setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\), derive the rate law \(\nu = k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]/(k_{-1} + k_2)\), and show that the pre-equilibrium result is recovered when \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\).

5.3 A Second Example: A ⇌ B, B + C → D

To see how the SSA can produce rate laws with no simple reaction order, consider a different mechanism:

\[\mathrm{A} \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{B} \qquad \mathrm{B} + \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{D}\]

Here, A isomerises reversibly to form a reactive intermediate B, which then reacts with a second species C to give the product D. The overall rate of product formation is \(\nu = k_2[\mathrm{B}][\mathrm{C}]\), but since B is a reactive intermediate we cannot measure \([\mathrm{B}]\) directly. We need to express \(\nu\) in terms of the stable, measurable species A and C.

The rate equation for B is:

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_1[\mathrm{A}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{B}] - k_2[\mathrm{B}][\mathrm{C}]\]

Setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\) and solving for \([\mathrm{B}]_\mathrm{ss}\):

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}] = [\mathrm{B}](k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}])\]

\[[\mathrm{B}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1[\mathrm{A}]}{k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]}\]

Substituting into the rate of product formation:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = k_2[\mathrm{B}]_\mathrm{ss}[\mathrm{C}] = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{C}]}{k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]} \tag{5.2} \end{equation}\]

This rate law has a striking feature: \([\mathrm{C}]\) appears in both the numerator and the denominator. As a result, the rate is not simply proportional to \([\mathrm{C}]\) — there is no well-defined order with respect to C. (The rate is, however, always first order in A.) This is a good example of a mechanism that gives rise to a rate law that cannot be expressed as a simple power law \(\nu = k[\mathrm{A}]^m[\mathrm{C}]^n\).

5.3.1 Limiting Cases

Once again, the denominator controls the behaviour, but now the comparison is between \(k_{-1}\) and \(k_2[\mathrm{C}]\) — and this depends on the concentration of C, not just on rate constants.

When \(k_{-1} \gg k_2[\mathrm{C}]\) (pre-equilibrium limit):

The denominator is dominated by \(k_{-1}\):

\[(k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]) \approx k_{-1}\]

Substituting into Eqn. (5.2):

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{C}]\]

The reaction is first order in A, first order in C, and second order overall. Physically, B reverts to A many times before it encounters a molecule of C. The first step is at equilibrium, and the second step is rate-determining. The effective rate constant is \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1} = Kk_2\), where \(K = k_1/k_{-1}\) is the equilibrium constant for the first step.

When \(k_2[\mathrm{C}] \gg k_{-1}\) (irreversible limit):

The denominator is dominated by \(k_2[\mathrm{C}]\):

\[(k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]) \approx k_2[\mathrm{C}]\]

Substituting into Eqn. (5.2):

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{C}]}{k_2[\mathrm{C}]} = k_1[\mathrm{A}]\]

The reaction is first order in A and zero order in C — the rate does not depend on \([\mathrm{C}]\) at all. This limit is reached whenever \(k_2[\mathrm{C}] \gg k_{-1}\), either because the concentration of C is large or because \(k_2\) itself is large. In either case, every molecule of B that forms reacts with C almost immediately, and the bottleneck is the rate at which B is produced from A.

Notice that because the comparison involves the product \(k_2[\mathrm{C}]\) rather than just a rate constant, it is possible to switch between these two regimes experimentally by varying \([\mathrm{C}]\). At low \([\mathrm{C}]\), where \(k_{-1} \gg k_2[\mathrm{C}]\), the reaction is first order in C and second order overall. At high \([\mathrm{C}]\), where \(k_2[\mathrm{C}] \gg k_{-1}\), the reaction becomes zero order in C and first order overall. The same mechanism produces different apparent rate laws depending on the conditions — a situation that would be difficult to interpret without the SSA framework. We will meet this same mathematical pattern again in the Lindemann mechanism (Lecture 7) and in enzyme kinetics (Lecture 8).

You should be able to: Apply the SSA to a mechanism with a reactive intermediate (such as A ⇌ B, B + C → D), derive the resulting rate law, and identify the limiting cases by examining which terms dominate in the denominator.

5.4 SSA versus Pre-Equilibrium

We have now seen two methods for handling reactive intermediates: the pre-equilibrium approximation (Lecture 4) and the steady-state approximation. How do they compare?

The pre-equilibrium approximation assumes that the first step genuinely reaches equilibrium before the slow step proceeds. This requires \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\): the reverse reaction of the first step must be much faster than the forward reaction of the second step. It gives a simple derivation and a clear physical picture, but applies only in this specific limit.

The steady-state approximation assumes only that the intermediate’s concentration reaches a quasi-steady value — which can happen whether or not the first step is at equilibrium. For mechanisms involving reactive intermediates, the SSA covers a broader range of conditions and reduces to the pre-equilibrium result when \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\).

In practice: if you are told (or can justify) that the first step is a fast equilibrium, the pre-equilibrium approach is quicker and gives the same answer. If no such information is given, or if you need a result that covers different limiting cases, use the SSA.

5.5 Key Concepts

  • The steady-state approximation (SSA) sets \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\) for a reactive intermediate I. This does not mean \([\mathrm{I}] = 0\) — it means \([\mathrm{I}]\) is approximately constant after a brief initial transient.
  • The SSA is valid when a reactive intermediate is consumed much faster than it is formed, so that its concentration remains small and approximately constant throughout the reaction.
  • Applying the SSA converts coupled differential equations — which generally have no analytical solution — into algebraic equations, allowing us to express the rate law in terms of stable, measurable species only.
  • For the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D, the SSA gives \(\nu = k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]/(k_{-1} + k_2)\), which contains both the pre-equilibrium limit (\(k_{-1} \gg k_2\)) and the irreversible limit (\(k_2 \gg k_{-1}\)) as special cases.
  • Some mechanisms produce rate laws where a species appears in both the numerator and denominator, giving no well-defined reaction order. The SSA naturally captures this behaviour.
  • The SSA is more general than the pre-equilibrium approximation and reduces to it as a special case. Use the SSA when you know (or can argue) that an intermediate is short-lived, but cannot justify a specific relationship between the rate constants.
