D Negative Reaction Orders

In Lecture 1, we defined the order of a reaction with respect to a given species as the exponent in the rate law. For the reactions we encounter in the main lectures, these exponents are positive integers or zero: increasing a reactant’s concentration either speeds up the reaction or has no effect. In some reactions, however, increasing the concentration of a particular species slows down the reaction. This unusual behaviour corresponds to a negative reaction order.

This appendix explores how negative orders arise from underlying mechanisms. The material is not examinable, but it illustrates an important theme of the course: macroscopic rate laws encode information about microscopic mechanisms.

D.1 What Does a Negative Order Mean?

When a species X appears with a negative order \(n\) in a rate law:

\[\nu = \frac{k[\mathrm{A}]^a[\mathrm{B}]^b}{[\mathrm{X}]^n}\]

increasing \([\mathrm{X}]\) decreases the reaction rate. The species X effectively acts as an inhibitor rather than a promoter of the reaction. Negative orders cannot arise from a single elementary step — they always signal a complex, multi-step mechanism in which the species interferes with one or more productive reaction pathways.

D.2 Example 1: Product Inhibition in H2 + Br2 \(\rightarrow\) 2HBr

The formation of hydrogen bromide — a chain reaction discussed in detail in Lecture 9 — provides a classic example of product inhibition. The empirical rate law is:

\[\nu = \frac{k[\mathrm{H}_2][\mathrm{Br}_2]^{1/2}}{1 + k'[\mathrm{HBr}]/[\mathrm{Br}_2]}\]

At high concentrations of HBr (specifically when \(k'[\mathrm{HBr}]/[\mathrm{Br}_2] \gg 1\)), this simplifies to:

\[\nu \approx \frac{k[\mathrm{H}_2][\mathrm{Br}_2]^{3/2}}{k'[\mathrm{HBr}]}\]

Here, HBr has an effective order of \(-1\). The very product we are trying to make inhibits its own formation.

The origin lies in the chain mechanism. The key propagation steps produce HBr via reactive H\(^{\bullet}\) radicals (Lecture 9), but HBr can also react with these same radicals:

\[\mathrm{H}^{\bullet} + \mathrm{HBr} \rightarrow \mathrm{Br}^{\bullet} + \mathrm{H}_2\]

This chain inhibition step consumes the very intermediates needed for product formation. The more HBr present, the more this reverse reaction competes with productive chain propagation, slowing the net rate. This is a general principle in chain kinetics: anything that consumes or deactivates chain-carrying intermediates produces negative kinetic behaviour.

D.3 Example 2: Competitive Adsorption in Surface Reactions

Surface-catalysed reactions provide another important class of systems with negative orders, closely related to the Langmuir adsorption model discussed in Lecture 8.

Unlike homogeneous reactions, surface processes require that reactants first adsorb onto the catalyst before they can react. The catalyst surface has a limited number of active sites, creating the potential for competition. For a typical surface reaction following the Langmuir–Hinshelwood mechanism:

  1. \(\mathrm{A}_{(\mathrm{g})} + {*} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{A}{*}\) (A adsorbs on an empty site \({*}\))
  2. \(\mathrm{B}_{(\mathrm{g})} + {*} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{B}{*}\) (B adsorbs on an empty site \({*}\))
  3. \(\mathrm{A}{*} + \mathrm{B}{*} \rightarrow \text{products} + 2{*}\) (reaction between adsorbed species)

The rate depends on having both A and B adsorbed on adjacent sites. If one species (say B) adsorbs much more strongly, or is present at very high pressure, it can monopolise the surface sites. Increasing the pressure of B then actually decreases the reaction rate, because B blocks the sites that A needs. This leads to a negative apparent order in B at high pressures, despite B being an essential reactant.

This site-blocking effect is commonly observed in industrial catalysis. For example, in CO oxidation on platinum, excess CO can poison the catalyst by blocking oxygen adsorption sites. The mathematical framework from Lecture 8 — where the Langmuir isotherm describes surface coverage as a function of pressure — provides the quantitative basis for understanding these effects.

D.4 Recognising Negative Orders

Negative orders often appear in disguised forms within rate expressions:

  • Denominator terms: Any concentration appearing in the denominator has a negative effective order, whether directly (e.g., \(k[\mathrm{A}]/[\mathrm{X}]\)) or within inhibition factors (e.g., \(k[\mathrm{A}]/(1 + K[\mathrm{X}])\)).
  • Saturation behaviour: Rates that plateau or decrease with increasing concentration suggest competitive effects leading to negative orders in the high-concentration limit.

The common thread is that negative orders arise when a species participates in the overall mechanism in ways that hinder rather than help the net forward reaction — whether by consuming reactive intermediates, blocking active sites, or driving reverse reactions.
