Lecture 5 The Steady-State Approximation

In the previous chapter, we used the pre-equilibrium approximation to derive a rate law for the mechanism A + B \(\rightleftharpoons\) C \(\rightarrow\) D, under the condition that the first step equilibrates much faster than the second step proceeds (\(k_{-1} \gg k_2\)). But what if this condition is not met? What if the intermediate C is consumed so rapidly that it never has time to reach equilibrium with the reactants? We need a more general method — one that works regardless of the relative magnitudes of the rate constants. This is the steady-state approximation (SSA).

5.1 The Idea Behind the Steady-State Approximation

A reactive intermediate is a species that is formed during a reaction and then consumed before the reaction is complete. It does not appear in the overall balanced equation. In many mechanisms, reactive intermediates are highly unstable: they are consumed almost as fast as they are produced, so their concentration remains small throughout the reaction.

The steady-state approximation exploits this observation. If an intermediate I is produced and consumed at similar rates, then after a brief initial period its concentration will reach a nearly constant value — a steady state. Mathematically, we express this as:

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]}{\mathrm{d}t} \approx 0\]

This does not mean that \([\mathrm{I}] = 0\). The intermediate is present — it must be, because the reaction proceeds through it — but its concentration changes so slowly compared to the other species that we can treat \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t\) as approximately zero. This single approximation converts a set of coupled differential equations into an algebraic problem that we can solve by hand.

The approximation is valid when the intermediate reaches its steady-state concentration quickly compared to the timescale of the overall reaction, and when the intermediate’s concentration remains small compared to those of the reactants and products. In practice, this means the intermediate must be reactive — it must be consumed rapidly by one or more subsequent steps.

Figure 5.1: When \(k_1 \ll k_2\) in a consecutive reaction A → B → C, the intermediate B remains at a low, nearly constant concentration throughout — a steady state. The SSA approximates this by setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]/\mathrm{d}t \approx 0\). [Draft figure from lecture slides.]

5.2 Worked Example: A + B ⇌ C → D

Let us apply the SSA to a mechanism we have already met:

\[\mathrm{A} + \mathrm{B} \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{D}\]

In Lecture 4, we analysed this mechanism using the pre-equilibrium approximation, which required \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\). The SSA makes no such assumption — it requires only that C is a short-lived intermediate whose concentration quickly reaches a steady state.

5.2.1 Setting Up the Rate Equations

The rate equations for each species are:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{A}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= -k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] + k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] \\ \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] - k_2[\mathrm{C}] \\ \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{D}]}{\mathrm{d}t} &= k_2[\mathrm{C}] \end{align*}\]

The intermediate C has two routes for consumption: it can revert to A + B (rate \(k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}]\)) or proceed to D (rate \(k_2[\mathrm{C}]\)). The competition between these two fates is what makes the general problem difficult — and what the SSA allows us to handle.

5.2.2 Applying the SSA

We apply the steady-state approximation to C by setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]/\mathrm{d}t \approx 0\):

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{C}] - k_2[\mathrm{C}] \approx 0\]

Rearranging to solve for the steady-state concentration of C:

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}] = (k_{-1} + k_2)[\mathrm{C}]\]

\[[\mathrm{C}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]}{k_{-1} + k_2}\]

The overall rate of product formation is then:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = \frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{D}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_2[\mathrm{C}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]}{k_{-1} + k_2} \tag{5.1} \end{equation}\]

This is the general SSA rate law for this mechanism. It is second order overall (first order in A, first order in B), with an effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/(k_{-1} + k_2)\).

5.2.3 Limiting Cases

The power of Eqn. (5.1) is that it contains the pre-equilibrium result as a special case, while also describing the opposite extreme. The behaviour is controlled by the denominator \(k_{-1} + k_2\) — specifically, by which term dominates.

Pre-equilibrium limit (\(k_{-1} \gg k_2\)):

When the reverse of the first step is much faster than the second step, \(k_{-1}\) dominates in the denominator:

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]\]

This is exactly the pre-equilibrium rate law we derived in Lecture 4 (Eqn. (4.3)). Physically, C reverts to A + B many times before occasionally proceeding to D. The first step has time to reach equilibrium, and the slow second step controls the overall rate. The effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\) depends on all three elementary rate constants.

Irreversible limit (\(k_2 \gg k_{-1}\)):

When the second step is much faster than the reverse of the first step, \(k_2\) dominates in the denominator:

\[\nu \approx k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]\]

Now the overall rate depends only on \(k_1\). Once C is formed, it proceeds to D so rapidly that reversion to A + B is negligible. We call this the irreversible limit — not because the first step is intrinsically irreversible (it still has a reverse rate constant \(k_{-1}\)), but because the fast second step (\(k_2 \gg k_{-1}\)) captures C before it has a chance to revert. The first step is made irreversible by the competition from the second step. The first step is therefore rate-determining, and the rate law is simply the rate of the first elementary step.

Notice that the two limits give the same functional form (both are second order in A and B) but different effective rate constants: \(k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\) in the pre-equilibrium limit and \(k_1\) in the irreversible limit.

Figure 5.2: Exact concentration profiles for the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D (solid lines), together with the SSA prediction for [D] (dashed line). The intermediate C remains at low concentration throughout, consistent with the steady-state assumption. The SSA prediction closely matches the exact solution except at short times, where the intermediate has not yet reached its steady-state concentration and the approximation is least valid.

You should be able to: Apply the steady-state approximation to the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D by setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{C}]/\mathrm{d}t = 0\), derive the general rate law \(\nu = k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]/(k_{-1} + k_2)\), and show that the pre-equilibrium result is recovered when \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\).

5.3 A Second Example: A ⇌ B, B + C → D

To see how the SSA handles a different type of mechanism — and to illustrate how it can produce rate laws with no simple reaction order — consider:

\[\mathrm{A} \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{B} \qquad \mathrm{B} + \mathrm{C} \xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{D}\]

Here, A isomerises reversibly to form a reactive intermediate B, which then reacts with a second species C to give the product D. The overall rate of product formation is \(\nu = k_2[\mathrm{B}][\mathrm{C}]\), but since B is a reactive intermediate we cannot measure \([\mathrm{B}]\) directly. We need to express \(\nu\) in terms of the stable, measurable species A and C.

5.3.1 Applying the SSA to B

The rate equation for B is:

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{B}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_1[\mathrm{A}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{B}] - k_2[\mathrm{B}][\mathrm{C}]\]

Setting this to zero:

\[k_1[\mathrm{A}] = [\mathrm{B}](k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}])\]

\[[\mathrm{B}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1[\mathrm{A}]}{k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]}\]

Substituting into the rate of product formation:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = k_2[\mathrm{B}]_\mathrm{ss}[\mathrm{C}] = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{C}]}{k_{-1} + k_2[\mathrm{C}]} \tag{5.2} \end{equation}\]

This rate law has a striking feature: \([\mathrm{C}]\) appears in both the numerator and the denominator. As a result, the rate is not simply proportional to \([\mathrm{C}]\) — there is no well-defined order with respect to C. (The rate is, however, always first order in A.) This is a good example of a mechanism that gives rise to a rate law that cannot be expressed as a simple power law \(\nu = k[\mathrm{A}]^m[\mathrm{C}]^n\).

5.3.2 Limiting Cases

Once again, the denominator controls the behaviour, but now the comparison is between \(k_{-1}\) and \(k_2[\mathrm{C}]\) — and this depends on the concentration of C, not just on rate constants.

When \(k_{-1} \gg k_2[\mathrm{C}]\) (pre-equilibrium limit):

\[\nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{C}]\]

The reaction is first order in A, first order in C, and second order overall. Physically, B reverts to A many times before it encounters a molecule of C. The first step is at equilibrium, and the second step is rate-determining. The effective rate constant is \(k_\mathrm{obs} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1} = Kk_2\), where \(K = k_1/k_{-1}\) is the equilibrium constant for the first step.

When \(k_2[\mathrm{C}] \gg k_{-1}\) (irreversible limit):

\[\nu \approx k_1[\mathrm{A}]\]

The reaction is first order in A and zero order in C — the rate does not depend on \([\mathrm{C}]\) at all. This may seem surprising, but the physical explanation is clear: when \([\mathrm{C}]\) is high, every molecule of B that forms reacts with C almost immediately. The bottleneck is the rate at which B is produced from A, so further increasing \([\mathrm{C}]\) has no effect. This is an example of saturation kinetics: the rate reaches a plateau at high \([\mathrm{C}]\) because the rate-determining step does not involve C.

This transition from second-order to saturation behaviour — where the reaction order with respect to a species changes depending on its concentration — is characteristic of mechanisms with competing fates for a reactive intermediate. We will encounter the same pattern again in Lecture 7 (unimolecular reactions) and Lecture 8 (enzyme kinetics).

5.4 SSA versus Pre-Equilibrium

We have now seen two methods for handling reactive intermediates: the pre-equilibrium approximation (Lecture 4) and the steady-state approximation. How do they compare?

The pre-equilibrium approximation assumes that the first step genuinely reaches equilibrium before the slow step proceeds. This requires \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\): the reverse reaction of the first step must be much faster than the forward reaction of the second step. It gives a simple derivation and a clear physical picture, but applies only in this specific limit.

The steady-state approximation is more general. It assumes only that the intermediate’s concentration reaches a quasi-steady value — which can happen whether or not the first step is at equilibrium. The SSA works across a broader range of conditions and reduces to the pre-equilibrium result when \(k_{-1} \gg k_2\).

In practice: if you are told (or can justify) that the first step is a fast equilibrium, the pre-equilibrium approach is quicker and gives the same answer. If no such information is given, or if you need a result that covers different limiting cases, use the SSA.

In Lecture 7, we will put the SSA to work on a real chemical problem — the Lindemann mechanism for unimolecular reactions — where it provides a beautifully simple explanation of how a molecule in a gas can acquire enough energy to decompose.

5.5 Key Concepts

  • The steady-state approximation (SSA) sets \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{I}]/\mathrm{d}t \approx 0\) for a reactive intermediate I. This does not mean \([\mathrm{I}] = 0\) — it means \([\mathrm{I}]\) is approximately constant after a brief initial transient.
  • Applying the SSA converts coupled differential equations into algebraic equations, allowing us to express the rate law in terms of stable, measurable species only.
  • For the mechanism A + B ⇌ C → D, the SSA gives \(\nu = k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{B}]/(k_{-1} + k_2)\), which contains both the pre-equilibrium limit (\(k_{-1} \gg k_2\)) and the irreversible limit (\(k_2 \gg k_{-1}\)) as special cases.
  • Some mechanisms produce rate laws where a species appears in both the numerator and denominator, giving no well-defined reaction order. The SSA naturally captures this behaviour.
  • Saturation kinetics — where the rate becomes independent of a reactant’s concentration at high concentrations — arises when the rate-determining step does not involve that reactant.
  • The SSA is more general than the pre-equilibrium approximation and reduces to it as a special case. Use the SSA when the relative magnitudes of rate constants are unknown or when a general result is needed.
