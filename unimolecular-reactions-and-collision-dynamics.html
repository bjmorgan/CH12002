Lecture 7 Unimolecular Reactions and Collision Dynamics

The gas-phase decomposition of azomethane follows first-order kinetics — and yet the molecules need collisions to acquire enough energy to react. Other reactions have rate laws containing three concentration terms, as if three molecules must collide at the same instant. These observations do not fit the simple picture of elementary reactions that we built up in earlier chapters, and explaining them will require the pre-equilibrium and steady-state tools we developed in Lectures ?? and ??.

7.1 The Lindemann–Hinshelwood Mechanism

At high pressures, the gas-phase decomposition of azomethane,

\[\mathrm{CH_3N_2CH_3} \rightarrow \mathrm{C_2H_6} + \mathrm{N_2}\]

follows first-order kinetics:

\[-\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{CH_3N_2CH_3}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k[\mathrm{CH_3N_2CH_3}]\]

At first sight, this seems entirely unremarkable. The stoichiometry involves a single reactant molecule falling apart, and the simplest possible mechanism is a single unimolecular elementary step: the molecule spontaneously decomposes. A unimolecular elementary step is first order, consistent with the data.

But there is a problem with this picture. A molecule cannot simply fall apart on its own — it needs enough energy to break a bond. We know empirically that the rate of this reaction increases with temperature, confirming that the molecules must overcome an energy barrier. In the gas phase, the only way a molecule can acquire that energy is through collisions with its neighbours.

This is where the contradiction emerges. If collisions supply the activation energy, the rate should depend on how often a molecule collides with another molecule. Collision frequency is proportional to the concentrations of both colliding partners, giving second-order kinetics (\(\nu = k[\mathrm{A}]^2\)), not first order. The observed first-order kinetics and the need for collisional activation seem to be incompatible. Something more subtle must be going on.

7.1.1 Separating Activation from Reaction

We need a mechanism that is consistent with both observations: the reaction requires collisional activation, yet the kinetics are first order at high pressures. In 1921, Frederick Lindemann proposed a mechanism that separates the activation event (a collision) from the reaction event (unimolecular decomposition):

\[\begin{align} \mathrm{A} + \mathrm{M} &\xrightarrow{k_1} \mathrm{A^*} + \mathrm{M} & &\text{(excitation by collision)} \tag{7.1} \\ \mathrm{A^*} + \mathrm{M} &\xrightarrow{k_{-1}} \mathrm{A} + \mathrm{M} & &\text{(deactivation by collision)} \tag{7.2} \\ \mathrm{A^*} &\xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{P} & &\text{(unimolecular decomposition)} \tag{7.3} \end{align}\]

Here, M represents any collision partner (often the reactant molecule itself), and A* represents an energetically excited molecule that has acquired enough energy to react. Notice that activation and reaction are separate events. A molecule can be activated by one collision, then react at a later time — or it may lose its energy in another collision before it has a chance to react.

7.1.2 The Lindemann Rate Law

The excited molecule A* is a reactive intermediate — it is formed by excitation (Eqn. (7.1)) and consumed by deactivation and decomposition (Eqns. (7.2)–(7.3)). At any instant, a small fraction of A molecules will have been excited by collisions, but most of these rapidly lose their energy again before they decompose. The concentration of A* therefore remains small and approximately constant after an initial transient, so we can apply the SSA. Setting \(\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{A^*}]/\mathrm{d}t \approx 0\):

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}[\mathrm{A^*}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}] - k_{-1}[\mathrm{A^*}][\mathrm{M}] - k_2[\mathrm{A^*}] \approx 0\]

Solving for \([\mathrm{A^*}]\):

\[[\mathrm{A^*}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}]}{k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}] + k_2}\]

The overall rate of product formation is then:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = k_2[\mathrm{A^*}]_\mathrm{ss} = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}]}{k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}] + k_2} \tag{7.4} \end{equation}\]

This is the Lindemann rate law. It has the same mathematical structure as the SSA rate laws we derived in Lecture ?? — a ratio with competing terms in the denominator, whose behaviour depends on which term dominates.

For azomethane, the reactant itself acts as the collision partner, so M = A and the rate law becomes:

\[\nu = \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}]^2}{k_{-1}[\mathrm{A}] + k_2}\]

The concentration \([\mathrm{A}]\) appears in both the numerator and the denominator, so the apparent reaction order depends on the pressure.

7.1.3 Pressure Dependence

Let us work through the two limiting cases of the general rate law (Eqn. (7.4)).

At high pressures, \([\mathrm{M}]\) is large, so the deactivation term in the denominator dominates: \(k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}] \gg k_2\). We can drop \(k_2\) from the denominator, and \([\mathrm{M}]\) cancels between numerator and denominator:

\[\begin{equation} \nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}]}{k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}]} = \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{A}] \tag{7.5} \end{equation}\]

The rate depends only on \([\mathrm{A}]\) — the reaction is first order, with an effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{eff} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\). This is consistent with the observed first-order kinetics of azomethane at high pressures.

Physically, an excited molecule undergoes many collisions before it has a chance to decompose. Excitation and deactivation reach equilibrium on a timescale much shorter than the decomposition, so the concentration of A* is maintained at its equilibrium value throughout the reaction. The rate-determining step is the slow unimolecular decomposition of A*.

At low pressures, \([\mathrm{M}]\) is small, so the decomposition term dominates: \(k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}] \ll k_2\). We can drop \(k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}]\) from the denominator, and \(k_2\) cancels:

\[\begin{equation} \nu \approx \frac{k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}]}{k_2} = k_1[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}] \tag{7.6} \end{equation}\]

The rate now depends on both \([\mathrm{A}]\) and \([\mathrm{M}]\) — the reaction is second order. Once a molecule is excited, it decomposes before it encounters another molecule that could deactivate it. Every excitation leads to reaction, so the rate-determining step is the excitation collision itself. This prediction was experimentally verified in 1927, providing strong support for the Lindemann mechanism.

The Lindemann mechanism thus explains the apparent contradiction we started with: the reaction is activated by collisions, but at high pressures excitation and deactivation equilibrate so rapidly that they are invisible to the experimenter. The observed kinetics reflect only the slow unimolecular decomposition step. Figure 7.1 summarises the transition between these two regimes.

Figure 7.1: Pressure dependence of the effective rate constant predicted by the Lindemann mechanism, plotted as \(k_\mathrm{eff}/k_\infty\) vs \([\mathrm{M}]/[\mathrm{M}]_{1/2}\) on logarithmic axes. At high pressure (right), excitation and deactivation equilibrate and the reaction is first order with \(k_\mathrm{eff} = k_\infty = k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\). At low pressure (left), every excitation leads to reaction and the kinetics become second order, with \(k_\mathrm{eff}\) proportional to \([\mathrm{M}]\) (dashed line, slope 1). The half-saturation concentration \([\mathrm{M}]_{1/2} = k_2/k_{-1}\) marks the crossover between the two regimes.

7.2 Third-Order Reactions and Collision Dynamics

Can a multi-step mechanism also explain rate laws that seem to require three molecules to collide at once?

7.2.1 The NO Oxidation

The oxidation of nitric oxide,

\[2\mathrm{NO} + \mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow 2\mathrm{NO_2}\]

has the experimentally determined rate law:

\[\nu = k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{O_2}]\]

The rate law contains three concentration terms, giving an overall order of three. The stoichiometry is consistent with a single termolecular step — two NO molecules and one O2 molecule coming together simultaneously. But the probability of three molecules arriving at the same point at the same instant is negligibly small compared to a bimolecular collision. A termolecular elementary step cannot account for the observed rate. Third-order kinetics must instead arise from a sequence of bimolecular steps.

A single bimolecular step produces at most second-order kinetics, so we need at least two steps. Since the rate law contains \([\mathrm{NO}]^2\), a pre-equilibrium mechanism with NO dimerisation as the fast first step accounts for this:

\[\mathrm{NO} + \mathrm{NO} \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} (\mathrm{NO})_2\] \[(\mathrm{NO})_2 + \mathrm{O_2} \xrightarrow{k_2} 2\mathrm{NO_2}\]

The approach to equilibrium in the first step is fast compared to the second step, so we can write:

\[K = \frac{k_1}{k_{-1}} = \frac{[(\mathrm{NO})_2]}{[\mathrm{NO}]^2}\]

The overall rate is determined by the slow step:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = k_2[(\mathrm{NO})_2][\mathrm{O_2}] = k_2 K[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{O_2}] \tag{7.7} \end{equation}\]

The \([\mathrm{NO}]^2\) factor enters through the equilibrium expression, and \([\mathrm{O_2}]\) from the slow step, giving overall third-order behaviour from two sequential bimolecular steps.

This mechanism also accounts for an unusual feature of this reaction: the rate decreases with increasing temperature. The effective rate constant is \(k_\mathrm{eff} = k_2 K\), a product of two terms that respond to temperature in opposite ways. The rate constant \(k_2\) for the slow step increases with temperature, as we would normally expect. But the dimerisation equilibrium is exothermic, so increasing the temperature shifts it to the left (Le Chatelier’s principle), reducing \(K\). The decrease in \(K\) outweighs the increase in \(k_2\), and the net effect is that \(k_\mathrm{eff}\) decreases with temperature.

7.2.2 Formation of Ozone: Why a Third Body is Needed

In the NO oxidation, the third-order kinetics arise because two NO molecules must come together to form the dimer before O2 can react — so \([\mathrm{NO}]^2\) enters through the pre-equilibrium expression. Ozone formation is also third order, but for a completely different physical reason — one that reveals a fundamental constraint on how small molecules can combine:

\[\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O_2} + \mathrm{M} \rightarrow \mathrm{O_3} + \mathrm{M}\]

with rate law:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = k[\mathrm{O}][\mathrm{O_2}][\mathrm{M}] \tag{7.8} \end{equation}\]

where M represents a third body — any molecule present in the gas (typically N2 or O2). The reaction involves only two chemical species combining (O and O2), so we might expect simple bimolecular kinetics. Why is a third body needed?

The requirement arises from the conservation of energy and momentum in molecular collisions (Figure 7.2). The formation of ozone is highly exothermic, releasing approximately \(107\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}\) when the new O–O bond forms. A bimolecular collision between O and O2 produces a vibrationally excited ozone molecule, O3*, that contains all of this excess energy in its vibrational modes.

Why can’t this energy simply leave as translational kinetic energy of the product? Because conservation of momentum fixes the product’s velocity. When O and O2 collide and stick together, the velocity of the resulting O3 is completely determined by conservation of momentum — there is only one product, so there is only one unknown and one equation. Once the velocity is fixed, so is the translational kinetic energy. All of the bond energy that was released must therefore end up as internal vibration. The resulting O3* is so highly excited that it rapidly dissociates back to O + O2 — unless something removes the excess energy first.

Figure 7.2: Schematic of the energy and momentum constraints in ozone formation. A bimolecular O + O2 collision produces a vibrationally excited O3* that retains the bond-formation energy as internal vibration. Without a third body to carry away this energy, O\(_3^*\) dissociates back to reactants.

This is where the third body becomes essential. When the vibrationally excited O3* collides with M, the excess energy can be transferred to M as translational kinetic energy, allowing the ozone molecule to stabilise:

\[\begin{align*} \mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O_2} &\underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{O_3^*} & &\text{(formation of excited ozone)} \\ \mathrm{O_3^*} + \mathrm{M} &\xrightarrow{k_2} \mathrm{O_3} + \mathrm{M} & &\text{(collisional stabilisation)} \end{align*}\]

Does the pre-equilibrium approximation apply to O3*? The excited intermediate dissociates on a timescale of just a few molecular vibrations — far faster than it encounters a third body M. Formation and dissociation of O3* therefore equilibrate long before any significant stabilisation occurs, so we can write:

\[K = \frac{k_1}{k_{-1}} = \frac{[\mathrm{O_3^*}]}{[\mathrm{O}][\mathrm{O_2}]}\]

The overall rate of stable ozone formation is determined by the slow stabilisation step:

\[\nu = k_2[\mathrm{O_3^*}][\mathrm{M}] = k_2 K [\mathrm{O}][\mathrm{O_2}][\mathrm{M}] = \frac{k_1 k_2}{k_{-1}}[\mathrm{O}][\mathrm{O_2}][\mathrm{M}]\]

This reproduces the observed third-order rate law (Eqn. (7.8)), with \(k_\mathrm{eff} = k_1 k_2/k_{-1}\).

The necessity for a third body illustrates a general principle: highly exothermic reactions involving small molecules often cannot proceed as simple bimolecular encounters. A triatomic like O3 has only three vibrational modes, so the bond energy is concentrated into just a few motions — enough to break the molecule apart again. A larger molecule, by contrast, can spread the same energy across dozens of vibrational modes, each receiving only a small share, and can survive long enough to lose the energy gradually through subsequent collisions. This is why third-order kinetics are common in atmospheric chemistry and combustion, where small radicals and atoms frequently combine, but rare for reactions that produce larger molecules. Appendix B explores this distinction further.

7.3 Key Concepts

  • The Lindemann–Hinshelwood mechanism explains how a collision-activated reaction can show first-order kinetics: activation occurs by collision, but at high pressures excitation and deactivation equilibrate rapidly, making only the slow unimolecular decomposition visible.
  • Applying the SSA to A* gives the Lindemann rate law, \(\nu = k_1 k_2[\mathrm{A}][\mathrm{M}]/(k_{-1}[\mathrm{M}] + k_2)\), which predicts a transition from first-order to second-order kinetics as the pressure decreases.
  • Third-order rate laws do not imply termolecular collisions. Pre-equilibrium mechanisms involving two sequential bimolecular steps can produce apparent third-order kinetics.
  • The negative temperature dependence of the NO oxidation arises because the effective rate constant \(k_\mathrm{eff} = k_2 K\) is a product of two competing terms: \(k_2\) increases with temperature, but the equilibrium constant \(K\) for the exothermic dimerisation decreases, and this decrease dominates.
  • Highly exothermic association reactions often require a third body to carry away excess energy from a vibrationally excited intermediate. Without this energy transfer, the product dissociates back to reactants.
